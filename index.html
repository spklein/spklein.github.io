Roots for crop improvement

Though they are the main plant organ responsible for water and nutrient uptake, roots are often an under-exploited breeding target for abiotic stress tolerance.

The structure, shape, and distribution of roots within the soil profile contribute to the plant's survival, particularly when necessary resources, like water and other nutrients, are available in limited quantities. Studying roots may offer novel ways to boost crop productivity in less fertile soils as well as increase sustainability practices in high-input agricultural systems.

My work seeks to identify the fitness optima associated with various root phenotypes in order to better understand the range of environments in which those phenotypes are advantageous. I am also interested in the genomic basis of root responses to a changing environment, particularly in those with limited resources.

About Me

I grew up at the edge of Chicago's suburbia, where neighborhood subdivisions transitioned to family farms. As an undergrad at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, I had the unique experience of working at the SoyFACE facility where I saw firsthand how climate change - elevated carbon dioxide, heat, drought, and ozone - will affect our crops in the coming decades. That experience was also the first time I was introduced to the wild world belowground.

I began my graduate degree at Penn State University in 2014 as part of the Roots Lab. My dissertation work was multi-disciplinary as I harnessed various methodologies to study maize root hydraulic traits and trait combinations that mitigated drought stress.

Shared genetic architecture underlying root metaxylem phenotypes under drought stress in cereals

Using GWAS and a genome-wide co-expression network, we detect several candidate genes associated with root metaxylem traits under optimal and drought stress in maize and rice. Pre-print

Multiple integrated root phenotypes are associated with improved drought tolerance

We identify root trait combinations that likely enable greater soil exploration by reallocating internal resources to greater root construction, restrict uptake of water to conserve soil moisture, and improve penetrability of hard, dry soils. Manuscript
